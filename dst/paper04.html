Sensitivity Analysis of Operator-Specific Crossover and Mutation Rates for Multi-Robot Path Planning Using NSGA-II: OFAT Analysis and Taguchi-Selected Combined Profile

Ariadie Chandra Nugraha1,2, Oyas Wahyunggoro1*, Adha Imam Cahyadi1

1 Dept. of Electrical Engineering and Information Technology, FT, Universitas Gadjah Mada
2 Dept. of Electrical Engineering Education, Universitas Negeri Yogyakarta
* Corresponding Author

Abstract

This paper presents a two-phase empirical sensitivity study of six operator-specific activation probabilities in an NSGA-II framework for three-agent grid-based multi-robot path planning. The operators under investigation are two crossover operators, whole-path swap (WPS) and partial-path swap (PPS), and four mutation operators: insert-zero (INS), delete-zero (DEL), direction-swap (SWD), and wait-detour (WTD). All six operators are domain-specific and feasibility-aware.

Phase 1 employs a One-Factor-At-A-Time (OFAT) design, sweeping each operator probability across 11 discrete levels on three 25×25 map topologies (S1: open, S2: bottleneck, S3: warehouse), totaling 1,980 runs. A conditional testing design is introduced to handle the structural dependency between INS and DEL. Phase 2 applies a Taguchi L18 orthogonal array (540 runs) to identify a cross-scenario combined parameter profile, subsequently validated in a 30-run confirmation experiment.

Key results: SWD exhibits the strongest and most consistent sensitivity (Kruskal-Wallis p < 0.001 in all three scenarios), with a clear inverted-U response peaking at rate 0.2–0.3 (improvements of +32% to +76% over the A*-initialized baseline). PPS achieves the highest single-scenario gain (+79% on S3) without the population-collapse effect observed in WPS at rate = 1.0. The Taguchi phase yields the Borda-selected combined profile: WPS = 0.4, PPS = 0.5, INS = 0.1, DEL = 0.3, SWD = 0.4, and WTD = 0.0, confirmed within the ±0.5 dB Taguchi tolerance in all scenarios (S/N gaps of −0.009 dB, −0.104 dB, and −0.023 dB).

1. Introduction

Path planning for multiple autonomous robots in shared environments presents a fundamental combinatorial challenge: each robot must navigate from its start position to its designated goal while avoiding static obstacles and spatiotemporal conflicts with other agents [1]. Such coordinated multi-robot navigation underpins practical systems such as automated-guided-vehicle (AGV) fleets on warehouse and industrial floors [2]. When formulated as a Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) problem that simultaneously minimizes individual robot travel times, the problem space grows exponentially with the number of agents, rendering classical planners insufficient [3].

Among evolutionary approaches, the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) has become a widely adopted framework for MOO in path planning due to its fast non-dominated sorting mechanism, crowding distance preservation, and ability to generate diverse Pareto-optimal solutions while maintaining computational tractability [7].

Despite this progression, a critical design question remains underexplored: what activation probability should each individual operator be assigned? Conventional GA literature often prescribes high crossover probability (Pc = 0.7–0.9) and low mutation probability (Pm < 0.1) based on standard binary-encoded GA theory [11]. However, these settings are tightly coupled to operator semantics and are not universally applicable for domain-specific, feasibility-aware operators [12].

2. Method

2.1 Problem Formulation

This study employs a per-agent multi-objective optimization framework in which individual robot travel times constitute separate optimization objectives, while goal achievement and inter-robot collision avoidance are enforced as hard constraints.

2.2 Chromosome Encoding

The chromosome concatenates discrete path sequences of all R robots into a unified decision vector. Each gene uses integer codes {0, 1, 2, 3, 4} representing no movement, up, down, left, and right respectively. The initial population is generated by applying the A* algorithm independently to each robot.

2.3 Genetic Operators

Whole-Path Swap (WPS): Exchanges the complete path segment of a randomly selected robot between two parent chromosomes.
Partial-Path Swap (PPS): Exchanges sub-segment suffixes at a random cut-point within each robot's path.
Insert-Zero (INS): Inserts a zero-valued gene (waiting action) at a randomly selected non-zero position.
Delete-Zero (DEL): Removes one zero from a sequence of consecutive zero-valued genes (excluding trailing padding).
Direction-Swap (SWD): Swaps two consecutive orthogonal movement actions, provided the resulting path remains obstacle-free.
Wait-Detour (WTD): Inserts an out-and-back temporal detour at a selected position.

2.4 Phase 1: OFAT Experimental Design

A controlled OFAT design isolates the individual contribution of each operator. A single operator's probability is swept across 11 discrete levels {0.0, 0.1, …, 1.0} while all other operators are disabled (P = 0.0). Each level is replicated with 10 independent random seeds, yielding 110 runs per operator-scenario block. All runs execute under a fixed computational budget of 100 generations.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1 OFAT Crossover Sensitivity

WPS: Kruskal-Wallis test indicates statistically significant rate-dependent variation for all three scenarios (S1: H = 79.9, p < 0.001***; S2: H = 34.8, p < 0.001***; S3: H = 31.1, p < 0.001***). Rate = 1.0 collapses to near-baseline performance due to rapid population homogenization. Recommended range: 0.1–0.4.

PPS: Significant for S1 (H = 23.3, p = 0.006**) and S2 (H = 19.8, p = 0.019*), but not for S3 (p = 0.30, ns) — the non-significance reflects uniformly high HV. At rate = 1.0, PPS maintains HV well above baseline (+31% to +79%). The S3 argmax is at rate = 0.8 (HV = 14,348, +79.2%). PPS consistently outperforms WPS.

3.2 OFAT Mutation Sensitivity

SWD shows the most consistent statistical significance among all operators: S1 (H = 89.7, p < 0.001***), S2 (H = 72.9, p < 0.001***), S3 (H = 53.7, p < 0.001***). It exhibits a clear inverted-U response peaking at rate 0.2–0.3 with improvements of +32% (S1), +46% (S2), and +76% (S3).

WTD: Strongly topology-dependent. Kruskal-Wallis not significant for S1 (p = 0.136, ns) and S2 (p = 0.079, ns), but highly significant for S3 (p < 0.001***). At rate = 0.1, S3 yields HV = 10,323 (+28.9%).

3.3 INS/DEL Structural Analysis

INS solo (P04-M1): Significant only for S1 (H = 20.03, p = 0.018*). INS* = 0.2 determined as the conditioning value via Borda score.

DEL conditional (P04-C1): Kruskal-Wallis not significant in any scenario, revealing a "threshold-active" pattern. In S3, active DEL rates provide ΔHV = +760–830 (+7.0–7.7%) over the INS-only baseline. Recommended: DEL = 0.3.

S3 achieves zero variance across all 10 replicates, indicating a deterministic outcome under the current setup.

4. Conclusion

This paper presented a two-phase empirical study of six operator-specific probabilities in an NSGA-II framework for three-agent grid-based multi-robot path planning. The completed experimental campaign comprises 2,550 runs.

Key findings:

  • SWD shows the strongest and most consistent sensitivity (KW *** in all scenarios), peaking at rate 0.2–0.3 (+32% to +76%).
  • PPS achieves the highest single-scenario gain (+79% on S3) without the rate=1.0 collapse observed in WPS.
  • WTD is topology-dependent: meaningful in S3 (+29% at rate=0.1) but negligible in S1 and S2.
  • DEL exhibits a "threshold-active" pattern: active rates provide +7.0–7.7% over INS-only baseline in S3, but the specific rate is secondary.
  • The Taguchi-selected Borda profile [MLLLHL] is confirmed within ±0.5 dB tolerance across all scenarios.
